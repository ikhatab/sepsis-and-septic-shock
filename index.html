Introduction to Shock and Sepsis

What is Shock?

Shock is a life-threatening condition of generalized inadequate blood flow to tissues, resulting in cellular dysfunction and eventual organ failure.

It is broadly categorized into four types based on primary pathophysiology:

  • Distributive: Reduced vascular tone (e.g., septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic shock)
  • Hypovolemic: Reduced circulating volume (e.g., hemorrhage, dehydration)
  • Cardiogenic: Pump failure (e.g., myocardial infarction, cardiomyopathy)
  • Obstructive: Physical obstruction to flow (e.g., pulmonary embolism, cardiac tamponade)

Sepsis and Septic Shock

Sepsis is defined as life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection (Sepsis-3 Consensus Definitions, 2016).

Organ dysfunction is identified as an acute change in total Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score ≥ 2 points consequent to the infection.

Septic Shock is a subset of sepsis in which underlying circulatory, cellular, and metabolic abnormalities are profound enough to substantially increase mortality.

Clinically identified by:

  • Vasopressor requirement to maintain MAP ≥ 65 mm Hg
  • Serum lactate level > 2 mmol/L after adequate fluid resuscitation

Epidemiology

Global Burden

Septic shock is a major global health burden:

  • Incidence: Millions of cases annually worldwide
  • In the U.S., sepsis is a leading cause of hospitalization and death
  • Age-adjusted incidence appears to be increasing
  • Mortality: Hospital mortality remains high (35-50%)
  • Higher mortality in patients with comorbidities, advanced age, and immunocompromised states

Epidemiological Data

Trends and Impact

Septic shock is the most common cause of shock in ICUs and consumes enormous healthcare resources due to:

  • Prolonged ICU and hospital stays
  • Need for advanced therapies
  • Long-term rehabilitation needs

Survivors often suffer from significant long-term physical, cognitive, and psychological impairments, known as post-intensive care syndrome (PICS) or post-sepsis syndrome.

Pathophysiology

Mechanisms of Septic Shock

Septic shock is a complex interplay between the pathogen and the host's immune response, leading to a cascade of events:

Immune Dysregulation and Inflammation

Pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) activate immune cells via pattern recognition receptors, triggering a massive release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and anti-inflammatory mediators.

Vasodilation and Distributive Shock

Inflammatory mediators induce expression of inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS), leading to excessive production of nitric oxide (NO), causing profound vasodilation and decreased systemic vascular resistance.

Myocardial Depression

Approximately 50-60% of patients develop septic cardiomyopathy, characterized by biventricular dilation, reduced ejection fraction, and decreased contractility.

Endothelial Dysfunction and Capillary Leak

The endothelium becomes activated and damaged, leading to increased permeability and capillary leak syndrome, exacerbating intravascular hypovolemia and tissue edema.

Microcirculatory and Mitochondrial Dysfunction

Blood flow at the microcirculatory level may remain impaired due to microthrombi, leukocyte plugging, and endothelial swelling. Mitochondrial dysfunction impairs cellular oxygen utilization, leading to cytopathic hypoxia.

Oxygen Delivery and Consumption

In septic shock, an imbalance between oxygen delivery (DO₂) and oxygen consumption (VO₂) occurs. When compensatory mechanisms fail, VO₂ becomes supply-dependent, leading to anaerobic metabolism and lactate production.

1. Pathogen Invasion

Sepsis begins with an infection. This can be caused by various microorganisms, including bacteria (most common), viruses (like influenza or SARS-CoV-2), fungi, or parasites. The infection can originate anywhere in the body, such as the lungs (pneumonia), urinary tract (UTI), abdomen, or skin. The pathogen itself and the toxins it may release are the initial triggers for the body's immune response.

2. Immune System Activation

The immune system detects the invaders by recognizing specific molecular patterns. These are called Pathogen-Associated Molecular Patterns (PAMPs) on the microbes, and Damage-Associated Molecular Patterns (DAMPs) released from injured host cells. Immune cells like macrophages and neutrophils are the first responders. They engulf the pathogens and release signaling molecules called cytokines to recruit more immune cells to the site of infection.

3. The Cytokine Storm

In a normal response, cytokine release is controlled. In sepsis, this regulation fails, leading to a "cytokine storm" --- a massive, uncontrolled release of pro-inflammatory cytokines like Tumor Necrosis Factor-alpha (TNF-α), Interleukin-1 (IL-1), and Interleukin-6 (IL-6). This overwhelming inflammatory response, intended to be protective, becomes harmful and spreads throughout the body, causing widespread systemic inflammation.

4. Effects on Vasculature

Cytokines, particularly nitric oxide induced by them, cause widespread vasodilation (widening of blood vessels). This leads to a significant drop in blood pressure (hypotension). Simultaneously, the blood vessels become more permeable or "leaky," allowing fluid to escape from the bloodstream into the surrounding tissues. This fluid shift causes edema (swelling) and further reduces the volume of blood circulating within the vessels.

5. Effects on Endothelium

The endothelium, the thin layer of cells lining the blood vessels, becomes damaged and activated by the inflammatory storm. This endothelial dysfunction impairs its ability to regulate blood flow and clotting. The body shifts into a pro-coagulant state, leading to the formation of tiny blood clots (microthrombi) in small vessels. These clots can obstruct blood flow, further starving tissues of oxygen.

6. Effects on the Heart

Sepsis directly affects the heart muscle, a condition known as septic cardiomyopathy. Inflammatory cytokines can depress the heart's ability to contract effectively. This myocardial depression, combined with the low blood pressure from vasodilation, leads to a reduced cardiac output, meaning the heart struggles to pump enough oxygenated blood to meet the body's needs.

7. Effects on Mitochondria

At the cellular level, sepsis causes profound mitochondrial dysfunction. Mitochondria are the powerhouses of the cell, responsible for producing ATP (energy). In sepsis, their function is impaired due to oxidative stress and direct inhibition by inflammatory mediators. This leads to a state called "cytopathic hypoxia," where cells cannot use oxygen effectively to generate energy, even if oxygen is being delivered. This is a primary driver of cellular injury and organ failure.

8. Multi-Organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS)

The culmination of hypotension, microthrombi, and cellular energy failure is widespread tissue hypoxia (lack of oxygen). This leads to damage and failure of vital organs. Common manifestations include Acute Kidney Injury (AKI), where the kidneys can no longer filter waste; Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS), where the lungs fill with fluid; and liver dysfunction. Sepsis is defined by the presence of this life-threatening organ dysfunction.

Septic Shock

Septic shock is the most severe form of sepsis. It is characterized by persistent, profound hypotension that does not respond to intravenous fluid administration alone and requires vasopressor medications to maintain adequate blood pressure. It represents a state of circulatory, cellular, and metabolic collapse, and is associated with a much higher risk of mortality.

Diagnosis

Clinical Assessment

History
  • Focus on symptoms of infection (fever, chills, rigors, cough, dysuria, abdominal pain)
  • Recent surgeries/procedures
  • Comorbidities and immunosuppression status
Physical Exam
  • General: Fever, hypothermia, altered mental status
  • Perfusion: Cold/clammy extremities, mottled skin, delayed capillary refill (>2 seconds)
  • Cardiovascular: Tachycardia, hypotension
  • Source Identification: Lung auscultation, abdominal exam, skin/soft tissue inspection

Biochemical and Laboratory Markers

Lactate

Marker of cellular stress and anaerobic metabolism

Level >2 mmol/L is key diagnostic and prognostic criterion

Procalcitonin (PCT)

Rises specifically in response to bacterial infections

Useful for supporting diagnosis and guiding antibiotic duration

Management

Initial Resuscitation & The "Golden Hour"

The first hour focuses on the "ABCs" and immediate life-saving measures:

Airway & Breathing
  • Assess for need for intubation
  • Use lung-protective ventilation if mechanical ventilation is required
Circulation
  • Obtain large-bore IV access
  • Begin IV fluid bolus (typically 20-30 mL/kg of crystalloid)
  • Draw blood work (lactate, cultures, etc.)
  • Start vasopressors if hypotension persists after initial fluid

Vasopressors and Inotropes

The goal is to restore adequate perfusion pressure (MAP 65-70 mm Hg as a starting point).

Agent Receptor Activity Dose Indication / Notes Key Evidence
Norepinephrine (First-line) α1 > β1 0.05-3.3 µg/kg/min First-line vasopressor. Increases MAP with minimal effect on HR and CO. Superior to dopamine in terms of mortality and arrhythmias
Vasopressin (Second-line) V1 > V2 0.03 U/min (fixed) Add-on to NE to reduce norepinephrine dose or achieve target MAP. VASST trial; VANISH trial. No clear mortality benefit, but norepinephrine-sparing.
Epinephrine β1, β2, α1 0.05-2 µg/kg/min Second-line alternative to NE or added to NE in refractory shock. No mortality difference vs. NE+vasopressin in meta-analyses.
Dopamine Dose-dependent 2-20 µg/kg/min Not recommended due to higher risk of arrhythmias and mortality compared to NE. SOAP II trial.
Dobutamine (Inotrope) β1 > β2 2-20 µg/kg/min Add if evidence of low cardiac output/scVO₂/low SvO₂ despite adequate fluid and MAP. Used in EGDT protocol; modern use is selective.

Antibiotic Therapy and Source Control

Timing and Selection
  • Administer effective IV antibiotics as soon as possible, ideally within 1 hour
  • Choose broad-spectrum empiric therapy based on suspected source and local antibiogram
  • Common regimens include:
    • Community-acquired: 3rd/4th gen cephalosporin + macrolide OR antipseudomonal β-lactam/β-lactamase inhibitor
    • Healthcare-associated/MDR risk: Antipseudomonal cephalosporin or carbapenem + second agent for Gram-negative coverage + agent for MRSA
De-escalation and Source Control
  • Re-assess at 48-72 hours and narrow spectrum based on culture results
  • Identify and control the source of infection within the first 12 hours if feasible
  • Methods may include:
    • Drainage of abscesses
    • Debridement of infected necrotic tissue
    • Removal of infected devices
    • Definitive control of a perforated viscus

Key Components of the Sepsis Bundle

Hour-1 Bundle
  1. Measure lactate, remeasure if initial lactate >2 mmol/L
  2. Obtain blood cultures before administering antibiotics
  3. Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics
  4. Begin rapid administration of 30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L
  5. Apply vasopressors if hypotensive during or after fluid resuscitation to maintain MAP ≥65 mm Hg
Benefits of Bundle Compliance
  • Standardized approach to sepsis care
  • Improved adherence to evidence-based guidelines
  • Reduced time to key interventions
  • Associated with improved survival rates
  • Facilitates quality improvement initiatives
